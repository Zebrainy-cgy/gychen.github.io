Statements

I am currently deeply interested in the topic of multiscale systems, both from a theoretical and applied perspective. My primary focus is on understanding the asymptotic behavior of these systems, and I am particularly keen on learning about theories related to mean-field theory and homogenization. These theoretical frameworks allow for a deeper analysis of multiscale systems across different scales.

Multiscale systems typically involve both temporal and spatial scales, which are often interdependent. Spatially, the scales range from the microscopic (atomic) level to the macroscopic (fluid) level. In microscopic systems, large particles exhibit Brownian motion due to collisions with smaller particles, which can be described by a Stochastic Ordinary Differential Equation (SODE). As the number of particles tends to infinity, the behavior of the system can be captured by a Stochastic Partial Differential Equation (SPDE). When the randomness of the system stabilizes, it can be described by a Partial Differential Equation (PDE), modeling the macroscopic phenomena.

I aim to analyze the progression from microscopic to macroscopic scales using asymptotic analysis, which is closely related to the principles of mean-field theory. Interestingly, this perspective can also be applied to neural networks. By considering each neuron as a particle, I hope to explore how the propagation of signals within a network can be understood through similar principles.

This line of research involves a substantial amount of knowledge related to Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), a field that I am passionate about. I am actively pursuing studies in this area, with the goal of applying these insights to the development of diffusion models.
